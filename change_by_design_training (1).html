Every decision made under pressure is a design decision. Today we examine the patterns those decisions create.

The Pressure Frame

Every week, leadership means
choosing between competing priorities

  • UrgencyvsSustainability
  • Customer demandvsTeam capacity
  • SpeedvsAlignment
  • OwnershipvsEscalation
  • Short-term winsvsSystem resilience

These aren't occasional dilemmas. They are repeated decisions — and repeated decisions determine where pressure lands.

Leadership Stress Test

Where did pressure land
in the last two weeks?

"What leadership moment did I rush, delay, or avoid last week?"

Take a moment — write it down privately.
Then ask: Who carried that pressure?

Leadership pressure rarely disappears. It shifts. It lands somewhere — and wherever it lands repeatedly, drift begins.

Drift Pattern 1 of 3

Bottleneck Drift

Pressure repeatedly lands on the same leaders and stays there because decisions, approvals, or clarity cannot move without them.

What causes it

  • Unclear decision boundaries
  • Trust gaps in delegation
  • Risk avoidance
  • Historical escalation habits
  • Leaders stepping in to protect outcomes
  • Lack of defined ownership

What the organization learns

  • Ownership sits above, not within teams
  • Escalation is rewarded
  • Decisions require approval
  • Speed depends on leader availability
  • Leaders become capacity constraints
Drift Pattern 2 of 3

Alignment Drift

Pressure appears because teams, departments, or leaders operate with different expectations, promises, timelines, or definitions of success.

What causes it

  • Lack of shared standards
  • Silo decision-making
  • Unclear handoff expectations
  • Speed prioritized over coordination
  • Communication gaps between leaders
  • Different definitions of "done"

What the organization learns

  • Promises may not reflect reality
  • Handoffs create risk
  • Clarity is negotiated, not designed
  • Escalation resolves alignment gaps
  • Friction becomes normalized
Drift Pattern 3 of 3

Standard Drift

Pressure leads leaders and teams to flex expectations, documentation, or quality standards in order to maintain speed.

What causes it

  • Urgency culture
  • Lack of visible standards
  • Process complexity
  • Time pressure
  • Absence of reinforcement
  • Speed rewarded more than consistency

What the organization learns

  • Standards are flexible
  • Shortcuts are acceptable
  • Clarity depends on the leader
  • Process is optional under urgency
  • Consistency is not protected
